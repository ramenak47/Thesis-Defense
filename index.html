The Illusion of Affordability
Does Buy Now, Pay Later framing anesthetize the pain of price volatility?

Problem

Two checkout technologies, one untested interaction

Dynamic pricing

  • Reference prices destabilized (Winer 1986)
  • Increases coded as losses — losses weigh ~2× gains (Kahneman & Tversky 1979)
  • Large unjustified increases read as unfair → anger, delay, switching (Xia et al. 2004)

Buy Now, Pay Later

  • Reliably lifts spending: +6.4% order size (Kumar et al. 2024)
  • ≈$130 at adoption, persistent (Di Maggio et al. 2022)
  • Mechanism contested: rational budgeting vs. affordability illusion
Volatility research assumes lump-sum payment. BNPL research assumes stable prices.
RQ1  Does installment framing mitigate sensitivity to price surges?   RQ2  If so — rational budgeting or affordability illusion?

Hypotheses

Three hypotheses, one ANOVA term each — and one rule committed in advance

H1 — Surge effectmain effect of Price
A price surge lowers purchase likelihood.
H2 — Anesthetic (buffer)Price × Payment
The surge penalty is smaller under BNPL than under lump-sum framing.
H3 — MechanismPrice × Payment × Reflection
H3a illusion: buffer vanishes under reflection (significant 3-way).  H3b rational: buffer persists (null 3-way).
Pre-committed rule: H3 is interpreted only if the reflection manipulation check fires; otherwise H3 is inconclusive — not disconfirmed.
Single confirmatory outcome: purchase likelihood. H1 and H2 rest on established effects; H3 is the ambitious mechanistic test.
Method

A 2×2×2 experiment with identical totals and a fixed confirmatory family

FactorLevels
Price context€299  vs.  €399  (+33% ≈ 3× latitude)
Payment frame€399 once  vs.  4 × €99.75, interest-free
Reflectionbudget-computation task vs. matched goals control

Same total payable in both frames — any difference is presentation.

Design & sample

  • Between subjects, 8 cells · N = 270 Prolific (Eurozone + US) · cells 32–35 · Mage = 34.7 · 76% BNPL-aware
  • Fictional product & retailer — standardizes brand attitudes and price knowledge
  • Browsing grid €279–€309 sets reference ≈ €300 · two-occasion cover story (checkout = return visit)

Analytic discipline

  • Registered analysis plan · single confirmatory DV: purchase likelihood (Dodds et al. 1991, α = .93)
  • One 2×2×2 ANOVA, Type III · Holm correction across H1–H3
  • Quality checks applied as flags, not deletions
Identical totals across frames = the invariance requirement of a framing study.

H1 — SUPPORTED

F(1,262) = 24.02, p < .001, ηp² = .084, d = 0.60, Holm < .001. Purchase likelihood 3.09 → 2.24.

H2 — NOT SUPPORTED

F = 0.52, p = .472. Penalty larger under BNPL (0.97 vs. 0.72). Buffer CI [−0.42, +0.93]: large buffers excluded, small ones not.

H3 — UNTESTED

F = 0.04, p = .849. No Price × Payment interaction under Goals (p = .707) or Financial (p = .522). No instrument, no buffer to remove.

No evidence of buffering — never “evidence of no buffer.” The reverse trend is descriptive: its test is the null interaction.

Interpretation & implications

A magnitude device cannot reach a legitimacy judgment

Salience captureAn unfair surge monopolizes attention — nothing left for the installment cue to grab.
Decoupling boundaryPartitioning a loss prolongs it: four installments = four reminders of a bad deal.
Owned bound33% confounds salience with unfairness by design ⇒ the null is bounded to large, unfair surges.
TheorySpending power ≠ sensitivity power — the two literatures cannot be assumed to share a mechanism.suggested
Management — matching principlePayment structure for affordability; justification & transparency for fairness.directional
PolicyNot a uniform anesthetic: price sensitivity intact; documented harms are mechanism-specific.directional
Not a buffer that failed — a buffer aimed at the wrong target.
Limitations · program · contribution

A boundary, a channel, and a program

  • Reflection instrument failed → H3 quarantined; touches nothing else.
  • Hypothetical paradigm → no real pain to numb; qualifies the null's generality, not its internal validity.
  • Floor compression → partial alternative; answered by convergence across uncompressed measures.
  • Narrower: one product category · willingness-to-wait failed as a measure · BNPL-literate panel.
Step 1Validate the reflection instrument under incentive-compatible stakes
Step 2Real-stakes buffer test with money on the line
Step 3Boundary map: surge magnitude × cost justification
Step 4Reversal test: installments as accommodation vs. financing add-on
A noticed installment frame did not weaken the effect of a noticed, unfair surge — and the resistance it failed to weaken runs through perceived fairness, a channel a magnitude device cannot reach.
